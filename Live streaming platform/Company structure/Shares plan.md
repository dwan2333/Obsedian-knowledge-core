### Part 2: The Board Seat Plan (The "3-Seat" Strategy)

The Board of Directors controls the day-to-day operations, fundraising, and executive firing. A two-person board is a trap that leads to 1-1 deadlocks.

**The Structure:**

- **Total Initial Seats:** 3
    
- **Seat 1:** Occupied by the CEO.
    
- **Seat 2:** Occupied by the CTO.
    
- **Seat 3 (The Tie-Breaker):** Remains **Empty**.
    

**The Rule:** Your Founder Voting Agreement will state that Seat 3 can _only_ be filled by a vote of the "Majority Class B Shareholder" (which is you). If you and the CTO ever deadlock on a critical decision, you instantly appoint a trusted advisor to Seat 3, giving yourself a 2-to-1 majority.

### Part 3: The Unvested Share Strategy (The "Ghost Founder" Shield)

You cannot hand the CTO 40% of the company on day one. Their equity must be earned through a **Restricted Stock Purchase Agreement (RSPA)** utilizing a standard 4-Year Vesting Schedule with a 1-Year Cliff.

**The CTO's Vesting Timeline (3,200,000 shares):**

- **Months 1 to 11:** 0 shares vested. If they quit to take another job, the "Company Repurchase Right" allows you to buy back all 3.2 million shares for their exact par value ($32.00 total).
    
- **Month 12 (The Cliff):** 800,000 shares (25%) instantly vest.
    
- **Months 13 to 48:** Approximately 66,666 shares vest every single month until the 4-year mark.
    

**The "Irrevocable Proxy" (The Power Strip):**

If the CTO quits in Year 2, they get to keep their _vested_ shares (e.g., 20% of the company). However, your Founder Voting Agreement will include an Irrevocable Proxy clause. The moment they cease full-time work for the startup, the voting power of those vested shares automatically transfers to the active CEO. They keep the financial value, but you take 100% of the voting control.

### Part 4: The Right of First Refusal (ROFR)

If the CTO leaves with vested shares, you must prevent them from selling that equity to a random third party, a competitor, or a hostile investor.

**The ROFR Rule:**

If the CTO finds a buyer who wants to purchase their vested shares for $5.00 a share, the ROFR legally forces the CTO to offer those shares to the Company (or to you directly) at that exact same $5.00 price first.

- If you want to clean up the cap table, you buy them back.
    
- If you don't have the cash, you simply decline, and only then are they allowed to sell to the outside buyer. This keeps you in total control of exactly who is on your cap table.
