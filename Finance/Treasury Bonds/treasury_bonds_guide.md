# Treasury Bonds — A Beginner's Guide

---

## 1. What Are Treasury Bonds?

Treasury bonds are debt securities issued by a national government (most commonly the U.S. government) to raise money for its spending. When you buy one, you are essentially **lending money to the government**. In return, the government promises to:

- Pay you **regular interest** (called a coupon).
- Return your **original investment** (called the principal or face value) when the bond matures.

They are considered one of the **safest investments in the world** because they are backed by the full faith and credit of the government.

---

## 2. The Three Types of Treasury Securities

| Type        | Maturity         | How It Pays You                                      |
|-------------|------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| **T-Bills** | Under 1 year     | No coupon. Sold at a **discount** to face value.     |
| **T-Notes** | 2 – 10 years     | Pays a **fixed coupon** every year.                   |
| **T-Bonds** | 20 – 30 years    | Pays a **fixed coupon** every year (longest term).    |

---

## 3. How T-Notes and T-Bonds Work (Coupon Bonds)

These are the most straightforward treasury securities.

**Example:**

- You buy a **$1,000 T-note** with a **4% coupon rate** and a **10-year maturity**.
- Every year, the government pays you **$40** in interest (4% × $1,000).
- After 10 years, the government returns your **$1,000** principal.
- **Total earned:** $400 in interest + $1,000 principal = **$1,400** over 10 years.

Simple and predictable — as long as you hold it to maturity.

---

## 4. How T-Bills Work (Zero-Coupon)

T-Bills do **not** pay a coupon. Instead, they are sold at a **discount** to their face value, and the difference is your profit.

**Example:**

- You buy a **$1,000 T-bill** for **$970**.
- It matures in 3 months.
- The government pays you the full **$1,000**.
- **Your profit:** $30 (the discount).

The return is baked into the price — no separate interest payments needed.

**Why people buy T-Bills:**

- **Safety** — A risk-free place to park cash for short periods.
- **Solid short-term yield** — Annualized rates can be 4–5%, which is competitive.
- **Cash management** — Banks, corporations, and funds use them to hold large sums safely.
- **Collateral** — Widely accepted as collateral in financial transactions and margin accounts.

Most T-Bill buyers are **not** speculators. They use T-Bills like a high-yield, government-backed savings account.

---

## 5. The Key Rule — Bond Prices and Interest Rates Move in Opposite Directions

This is the most important concept in the bond market:

> **Rates go UP → Bond prices go DOWN**
> **Rates go DOWN → Bond prices go UP**

### Why? A Step-by-Step Example:

**Scenario A — Rates Rise:**

1. You buy a $1,000 T-note with a **4% coupon** (pays $40/year).
2. A year later, the Fed raises rates. New T-notes now offer **5%** (pay $50/year).
3. Nobody wants your bond paying only $40/year when they can get $50/year from a new one.
4. To sell your bond, the price must **drop** — maybe to around **$920**.
5. At $920, your $40 coupon gives the new buyer a yield closer to the 5% market rate, making the deal fair.
6. **You lose money** if you sell early (you paid $1,000, sold for $920).

**Scenario B — Rates Fall:**

1. You still hold your $1,000 T-note with a **4% coupon** (pays $40/year).
2. Rates fall to **3%**. New T-notes only pay $30/year.
3. Now your bond paying $40/year is **more attractive** than anything on the market.
4. Buyers will pay a **premium** — maybe **$1,080** — for your higher-paying bond.
5. **You make a profit** if you sell early (you paid $1,000, sold for $1,080).

---

## 6. Two Choices as a Bondholder

| Strategy            | What Happens                                                                 |
|---------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Hold to Maturity** | Ignore all price swings. Collect your coupon every year. Get your $1,000 back at the end. You never lose money (assuming no government default). |
| **Sell Early**       | Sell on the secondary market before maturity. You could make a **profit** or take a **loss** depending on where interest rates have moved. |

---

## 7. How Traders Profit from the Treasury Market

Professional traders use several strategies:

**1. Price Movement (Directional Trading)**
If a trader believes interest rates will **fall**, they buy bonds now. As rates drop, bond prices rise, and they sell at a profit. If they expect rates to **rise**, they can short bonds and profit from the price decline.

**2. Yield Curve Trades**
Traders bet on how the gap between short-term and long-term rates will change. For example, a "steepener" trade profits when long-term rates rise faster than short-term rates.

**3. Carry Trade**
Traders earn the coupon income while holding the bond, often using borrowed money (leverage) to amplify returns. If a bond yields 4% and borrowing costs 3%, the trader pockets the 1% spread.

**4. Futures and Options**
Many traders use Treasury futures contracts to speculate on rate direction or hedge risk, without ever owning the actual bonds.

**5. Arbitrage**
Sophisticated traders exploit tiny price differences between related instruments — for example, the gap between a Treasury bond's market price and its futures contract price.

---

## Quick Recap

- **Treasury securities** = lending money to the government in exchange for a return.
- **T-Bills** = short-term, sold at a discount, no coupon.
- **T-Notes / T-Bonds** = medium to long-term, pay a fixed coupon.
- **Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions** — this is the foundation of all bond trading.
- **Hold to maturity** = safe and predictable.
- **Sell early** = potential profit or loss depending on rate movements.
- **Traders profit** by predicting rate changes, exploiting yield curves, earning carry, and using futures.
