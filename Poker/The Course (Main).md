## Branch 1 — The Poker Ecosystem

### Live Cash Games

The bread-and-butter format for most serious players. Games are stratified by stake level, and each tier has a distinct character:

| Stake | Level | Player Profile |
|---|---|---|
| **1-2 / 1-3** | Entry | Recreational players; loose, passive tendencies |
| **2-5 / 5-10** | Semi-Pro/Pro | More aggressive; tighter ranges; higher baseline skill |

- **1-2 / 1-3 games** are the most common entry point. They are filled with recreational players who play too many hands and rarely fold to pressure — this is your primary hunting ground as a developing player.
- The goal at small stakes is not to outplay opponents with elaborate bluffs; it is to **exploit weak ranges** by value-betting relentlessly.

### Online Cash Games

- **Hyper-competitive** at all stake levels compared to live games — players have access to solvers, databases, and coaching material.
- Games run at **high volume and speed**, rewarding players who can make sound decisions quickly.
- **Unlimited earning potential** — volume is unconstrained compared to live play.
- Not recommended as a starting point if you are building fundamentals; live 1-2 is more forgiving and more profitable per hand for an exploitative player.

### Tournaments

| Format | Character |
|---|---|
| Live Tournaments | Social; high variance; luck plays a larger short-term role |
| Online Tournaments | More forgiving variance over volume; faster feedback loops |

- Tournaments require a distinct skill set (ICM, stack-size adjustments, push/fold theory) that is separate from cash game fundamentals — Part I focuses on cash game strategy.

## Branch 2 — Core Strategy Philosophy

### Play the Course, Not the Players

The central premise of the book is that most small-stakes players are beatable with a **systematic, patient strategy** rather than with table reads and exploitative adjustments. Miller calls this "playing the course" — executing a sound game plan regardless of individual opponent personalities.

- **Attack weakness**: Most players at small stakes play **too many hands**. Wide ranges are weak ranges. Target pots where opponents are likely holding mediocre hands.
- **Avoid strength**: When opponents show **narrow, aggressive action** (raise → 3-bet → large turn bet), they have it. Fold. Do not hero-call.
- **Focus on intentional actions**: Make every bet and call deliberate, not reflexive. Ask: "What range am I targeting? What hands am I trying to get value from?"

### The Three Pillars

The philosophy reduces to three disciplines:

1. Enter pots in position with the right hands
2. Do not pay people off when they represent strong hands
3. Know when your hand is good enough to want a showdown

## Branch 3 — The Poker Pyramid

The **Poker Pyramid** is a visual model for how hand ranges narrow as streets progress. Understanding this model is foundational to everything that follows.

```
         River  ◄ Narrowest: only strong hands remain
        Turn
       Flop
    Pre-Flop    ◄ Widest: all starting hands in play
```

- At **pre-flop**, a player can enter with a wide range (any two cards, speculative hands, any pair).
- Each successive street — flop, turn, river — filters that range. Players who stay in are calling or betting with increasingly **defined, stronger holdings**.
- **Implication**: A large bet on the river carries enormous information. Most small-stakes players do not bluff rivers. If they are betting big and you only beat a bluff, you should fold.

## Branch 4 — Skill #1: Pre-Flop Strategy

### Hand Selection Criteria

Not all starting hands are equal. Miller organizes hand value around four properties:

| Property | What It Means | Example Hands |
|---|---|---|
| **Suitedness** | Equity when called; flush draws add value | A♠K♠ > AKo |
| **Big Card Value** | High cards connect with strong boards | AK, AQ, KQ |
| **Connectedness** | Straight draw potential | JT, 98, 87 |
| **Pocket Pairs** | Set-mining; high pairs for overpair value | AA–22 |

### Positional Frequencies (Raise-First-In)

Position is the most important variable in pre-flop hand selection. The later your position, the wider you can open because you will have positional advantage post-flop.

| Position | Approximate Open Frequency |
|---|---|
| **Early Position (UTG, UTG+1)** | ~14% |
| **Cutoff (CO)** | ~22% |
| **Button (BTN)** | ~33% |
| **Blinds** | Defend vs. steals; do not open-limp |

### The Golden Rule

> **Always raise if you are the first player entering the pot.** Never limp. Limping is a tell that you are unsure of your hand's value, and it surrenders initiative before the flop is even dealt.

## Branch 5 — Skill #2: Don't Pay People Off

This is the single most important leak to fix in small-stakes play. **Calling off stacks against large bets is where most players lose their money.**

### The Core Principle

- **Believe large turn and river bets.** At 1-2 and 2-5, players rarely bluff with large sizing on later streets. When they bet pot or overbet, they almost always have it.
- If you can only beat a bluff, fold. The bluff frequency at these stakes does not justify the call.
- Avoid the **"pot-committed" fallacy**: the money already in the pot is gone regardless of what you do. Your decision should be based on the **current equity** of your hand vs. their range — not on what you've invested.

### Decision Framework

| Opponent bets large on turn or river | Your action |
|---|---|
| You only beat bluffs | Fold |
| You beat a meaningful portion of their range | Call or raise |
| Board completed a draw and they overbet | Give credit; fold medium hands |

## Branch 6 — Skill #3: Assess Hand Value

### Good Hands Want Showdowns

A strong hand's goal is to **reach showdown without scaring opponents off**. Over-betting to "protect" your hand often folds out the exact weak holdings you want to get value from.

- If you have top pair top kicker on a dry board, **bet for value** — but bet a size that keeps weaker hands calling (½ pot, not overbet).
- **Extract value from weak ranges**: build pots gradually. Let opponents with dominated hands stay in.

### Recognizing When to Call vs. Fold

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| You have a strong hand; opponent bets small | Call or raise for value |
| You have a medium hand; opponent bets large on turn/river | Fold — you're likely beat |
| You have the best hand and opponent is passive | Value bet; don't slow-play |
| Board is scary (paired, flush-completed) and opponent bets big | Give credit; fold medium hands |

## Key Takeaways

- The small-stakes poker ecosystem rewards **patient, value-focused play** over complex bluffing strategies.
- The **Poker Pyramid** explains why range strength grows as streets progress — respect large late-street bets.
- **Pre-flop discipline** (raise first-in, respect position) sets up every downstream decision.
- **Not paying people off** is the single largest source of profit improvement for most developing players.
- **Assess hand value honestly** — a good hand wants showdowns; a mediocre hand facing a big bet should fold.
