# 1-2 Hand Quizzes

*Companion document to [Part 2 — Beating Live 1-2 Games](<Part 2 Beating Live 1-2 Games (Main).md>)*

*These quiz hands let you practice applying Skills #1–#3 before moving to Part 3. Read each scenario, decide on your action, then click to reveal Miller's answer.*

## How to Use

Each hand presents a scenario — stacks, position, and action up to your decision. Work out your choice before clicking to reveal Miller's recommended play and reasoning.

> [!info] Game context for every quiz
> You are playing in a **$1-$2 no-limit hold 'em** cash game with **$300 effective stacks**.

## Quiz Hands

### Quiz 1 — Suited connectors vs. limpers

> [!example] Quiz 1 — 9♣8♣ two off the button
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. You are two off the button. Two players limp to you.
> **Hand.** 9♣ 8♣
> **Question.** What do you do?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** Raise to about **$10 or $12**.
> > **Reasoning (Skill #1).** Limping forks your range into strong raising hands and weak limping hands, and makes you play just to "hit flops." 9-8 suited is a great bluffing hand — raising builds a pot you may steal on later streets and lets you play it like any other threatening hand when you flop a pair or draw.
> > **Takeaway.** Raise the hands you play. Don't look to "see a cheap flop" — that's the losing strategy your opponents are using.

### Quiz 2 — Offsuit broadway in the small blind

> [!example] Quiz 2 — K♣J♠ in the SB facing a raise + call
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. A player limps. The next player raises to $10. A third player calls. You are in the small blind.
> **Hand.** K♣ J♠
> **Question.** Fold, call, or 3-bet?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Fold.**
> > **Reasoning (Skill #1).** Offsuit hands are not good in this game. An early-position raise is a fairly good sign of strength, the raise size is "big," and you are out of position. The $1 dead in the SB is trivial compared to the $9 you have to call. The raiser's average hand is at least as strong as yours.
> > **Takeaway.** Fold most of your offsuit hands out of position against a raise.

### Quiz 3 — Weak suited ace vs. button steal

> [!example] Quiz 3 — A♣4♣ in the BB vs. button steal raise
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Two players limp. An aggressive regular makes it $12 to go on the button. The small blind folds. You are in the big blind.
> **Hand.** A♣ 4♣
> **Question.** Fold, call, or 3-bet — and what size?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Reraise to $35.** (Folding isn't crazy, calling is okay, but 3-betting is strongly preferred.)
> > **Reasoning (Skill #1).** Aggressive button raises over limpers are steal raises with hands as weak as 8-5 suited. Reraising balances your range with A-A/K-K, disguises your strategy, and paints them into a corner — they either fold pre-flop or call light and face a c-bet on most flops.
> > **Takeaway.** Attack obvious steal raises with a polarized 3-bet range. Expect high variance — it will blow up sometimes, and that's part of the plan.

### Quiz 4 — A-J offsuit vs. a small-blind 3-bet

> [!example] Quiz 4 — A♣J♠ on the button facing a 3-bet
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Three players limp. You make it $12 to go on the button. The small blind reraises to $40. The limpers fold.
> **Hand.** A♣ J♠
> **Question.** Fold, call, or 4-bet?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Fold.**
> > **Reasoning (Skill #1).** Against a balanced 3-betting range, A-J offsuit calls or even 4-bets fine. But 1-2 players don't 3-bet balanced — they 3-bet A-A, K-K, Q-Q, and maybe A-K or J-J. Against that range, A-J offsuit is a no-brainer fold.
> > **Takeaway.** Never assume your opponents play like you do. A 1-2 SB 3-bet is almost always a monster. Fold A-J offsuit.

### Quiz 5 — Weak suited king in early-ish position

> [!example] Quiz 5 — K♦5♦ two off the button
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Two players limp. You are two off the button.
> **Hand.** K♦ 5♦
> **Question.** What do you do?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Fold.**
> > **Reasoning (Skill #1).** Two off the button is "early position" in Miller's range chart, and K-5 suited is nowhere near those starting hands. Don't be seduced by "kind of late position" or "suited." Your edge comes from playing tighter than your opponents — hope is not a strategy.
> > **Takeaway.** Insist on tight starting ranges. A tighter range is "the gift that keeps on giving" — you'll consistently be out-kickering and out-flopping your opponents.

### Quiz 6 — Top pair when the flush comes and it's checked to you

> [!example] Quiz 6 — K♦Q♦ turn decision after flop flush card
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Four players limp. You raise to $15 from one off the button with K♦Q♦. The BB calls and three of the four limpers call (loose, call-happy table). **Pot $78, $285 behind.**
> Flop **Q♣ T♣ 7♦.** All check to you. You bet $70. Two players call. **Pot $288, $215 behind.**
> Turn **2♣** (flush card). Both opponents check.
> **Question.** Check or bet — and if bet, what size?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Shove all-in ($215).**
> > **Reasoning (Skill #3).** Flushes are hard to make — out of 1,035 possible holdings, only 45 make a flush, even if opponents play every suited hand. Loose callers on the flop can have any queen, ten, seven, straight-draw hand (A-K through 8-6), or flush draw. Most of the time no one has a flush. Bet your top pair for value.
> > **Takeaway.** In loose games, bet, bet, bet your top pairs. Don't let a scary card stop you from value-betting weak ranges.

### Quiz 7 — Top pair vs. a turn shove on the flush card

> [!example] Quiz 7 — K♦Q♦ facing a turn all-in on the flush card
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Identical to Quiz 6 up to the turn: four limpers, you raise to $15 with K♦Q♦ from one off the button, BB + three limpers call. **Pot $78, $285 behind.**
> Flop **Q♣ T♣ 7♦.** Checked to you. You bet $70. Two call. **Pot $288, $215 behind.**
> Turn **2♣.** The first opponent **shoves all-in for $215.**
> **Question.** Call or fold?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Fold.**
> > **Reasoning (Skill #2).** This is the bread-and-butter application. A big turn shove is an action your opponent takes with only a tiny slice of his range, and that slice is extremely strong. 1-2 players don't bluff big often enough to justify calling with one pair. Compare with Quiz 6: when he *checks*, the strong hands drop out of his range and only the weak hands remain — so you bet. When he *shoves*, only the strong hands remain — so you fold.
> > **Takeaway.** A check and a shove carry opposite information. Same hand, same board — the action tells you whether to attack or run. This is the core logic of superior no-limit play.

### Quiz 8 — Top pair on a paired board, turn decision

> [!example] Quiz 8 — Q♥J♥ turn decision on a paired flop
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Two players limp. You raise to $12 on the button with Q♥J♥. Blinds fold, both limpers call. **Pot $39, $288 behind.**
> Flop **J♦ 4♣ 4♥.** Checked to you. You bet $20. One limper calls.
> Turn **T♣.** Opponent checks.
> **Question.** Check or bet — and if bet, what size?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **It depends on your opponent.** Use streets-of-value logic.
> > - **Against irrational call-stations:** Bet turn and river, keeping sizes small (e.g., $40 turn, $60 river). They'll call down with any jack and even 8-8.
> > - **Against a rational opponent:** You can't get two streets of value, so check once. Leaning toward **check turn / bet river** — it denies you paying off trips if he bets a blank river (Skill #2 fold), and induces occasional river bluffs you also fold to.
> > - **Against a baffling loose opponent:** Bet turn, check river — denies a free card to 7-7 or A-Q improving.
> >
> > **Reasoning (Skill #3).** The turn T kills J-T (your best caller) and makes lower kickers (J-9, J-8) play their board-counterfeited kicker.
> > **Takeaway.** Sizing is opponent-dependent. There's no magic bullet — experiment and adjust.

### Quiz 9 — Pocket aces vs. a small probe bet on a draw-heavy flop

> [!example] Quiz 9 — A♣A♦ UTG facing a $15 BB probe bet
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. You open A♣A♦ under the gun to $10 (a standard raise size in your game). A player calls behind, the BB calls. **Pot $31, $290 behind.**
> Flop **J♣ 7♣ 5♦.** The big blind leads out for **$15** (an out-of-flow probe bet, not "checking to the raiser").
> **Question.** Fold, call, or raise — and what size?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Min-raise to $30** (calling is also acceptable; folding is not).
> > **Reasoning (Skill #2 + #3).** A small flop bet is *not* a Skill #2 fold situation — Skill #2 is for big turn/river bets. This is a probe bet, likely a hand like J-8 looking to find out cheaply. Raising big makes him fold; calling is ambiguous but invites the player behind. A min-raise to $30 shows a touch of strength but most players can't resist calling, keeps the live player behind in with a jack or draw, and starts streets-of-value mode. Then size turn/river for what a J-8 type would call.
> > **Takeaway.** Don't let small flop bets scare you off big hands. You're almost always ahead — the job is to extract while obscuring your strength.

### Quiz 10 — Bottom set facing a big turn all-in raise

> [!example] Quiz 10 — 4♣4♠ facing a turn all-in raise with bottom set
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. A player limps. You raise 4♣4♠ to $10 from four off the button. Two callers behind, BB calls, limper calls — 5 players. **Pot $51.**
> Flop **Q♣ J♣ 4♥** (bottom set). Checked to you, you bet $50. One player behind calls. **Pot $151, $240 behind.**
> Turn **A♦.** You bet $90. Opponent **raises all-in for $150 more.**
> **Question.** Call or fold?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Call — and don't think too hard about it.**
> > **Reasoning (Skill #2 doesn't apply here).** Skill #2 says fold when you can't beat what he's representing — but here you *can* beat a large part of his range (A-Q, A-J, Q-J, or an ace-high flush draw like A♣T♣ shoving to avoid a tough river). You're behind K-T and higher sets, but you have legitimate re-draws (board pair → boat) against the straight. When you beat some of his range *and* can draw out on the rest, it's a slam-dunk call.
> > **Takeaway.** Skill #2 is not automatic. When you have a big hand that beats part of his range and has redraws against the rest, play for stacks.

### Quiz 11 — Pocket kings facing a big flop check-raise in a 3-bet pot

> [!example] Quiz 11 — K♦K♠ facing a flop check-raise
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Two players limp. Another player raises to $12 in the cutoff. You 3-bet to $35 on the button with K♦K♠. Everyone folds except the cutoff, who calls. **Pot $77, $265 behind.**
> Flop **T♣ 4♣ 3♠.** Opponent checks. You bet $50. **Opponent check-raises to $150.**
> **Question.** Fold, call, or jam?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **Move all-in.**
> > **Reasoning (Skill #2 limitation).** Skill #2 applies most clearly on the turn and river — not the flop. You can be behind (T-T, 4-4, 3-3, A-A), but you're ahead of Q-Q, J-J, A-T, big flush draws with overcards like A♣Q♣ or K♣J♣, and combo draws like 6♣5♣. With $177 already in the pot and $215 left, you risk $215 to win $392 — you only need ~35% equity. In a 3-bet pot with an overpair when your opponent forces the issue on a dry-looking flop, often you just gamble.
> > **Takeaway.** Flop bets — even big ones — are not Skill #2 fold spots. With an overpair in a 3-bet pot, do the math on pot odds and commit.

### Quiz 12 — Top pair on a paired board facing a small river bet

> [!example] Quiz 12 — J♣T♣ facing a $30 river lead
> **Scenario.** $1-$2, $300 stacks. Three players limp. You raise J♣T♣ to $15 on the button. BB + two limpers call — 4 players. **Pot $63, $285 behind.**
> Flop **T♦ 9♣ 9♠** (top pair). Checked to you, you bet $40. One caller.
> Turn **A♥.** Opponent checks. You judge the ace kills your value and **check back**. **Pot $143, $245 behind.**
> River **3♣.** Opponent bets **$30.**
> **Question.** Fold, call, or raise?
>
> > [!success]- Click to reveal Miller's answer
> > **Recommendation.** **It depends on the opponent — all three options are defensible.** This is not a neat Skill #2 situation because the bet is tiny.
> > - **Call** against opponents who'd bluff a busted straight draw (K-Q, Q-J, 8-7) this small but size up with trips.
> > - **Fold** against opponents who never bluff and would check a ten — you might never win outright and only chop sometimes, failing the 15% equity threshold the $173-to-$30 odds require.
> > - **Raise** to fold out a lone ten or even a weak ace; they almost never fold trip nines but once in a blue moon fold trips-bad-kicker.
> >
> > **Reasoning.** Pot odds give you a 15% threshold — seemingly a snap call, but only if you ever actually win. Player type is everything.
> > **Takeaway.** Not every spot fits a skill. When it doesn't, use judgment, write the hand down, and analyze it later. Your long-term edge comes from making the unfamiliar familiar.

## Key Patterns Across the Quizzes

> [!summary] What the 12 quizzes drill
> - **Quizzes 1–5 — Skill #1 (Pre-flop):** Raise the hands you play (not limp). Fold offsuit junk out of position. Attack steal raises. Don't assume 1-2 players 3-bet balanced. Tight ranges are the gift that keeps on giving.
> - **Quizzes 6 & 7 — The core contrast:** Same hand, same board, opposite action. **Check = weakness (bet); shove = strength (fold).**
> - **Quiz 8 — Streets of value:** Which board cards kill which callers? How rational is villain? Size for the target.
> - **Quizzes 9, 10, 11 — Skill #2 boundaries:** Small bets, flop bets, and spots with redraws are **not** Skill #2 folds. Skill #2 is big turn/river bets only.
> - **Quiz 12 — When no skill fits:** Use judgment, write it down, study it later.
