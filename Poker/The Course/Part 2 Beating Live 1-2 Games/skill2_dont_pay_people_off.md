# Skill #2 — Don't Pay People Off

*Companion document to [Part 2 — Beating Live 1-2 Games](<Part 2 Beating Live 1-2 Games (Main).md>)*

---

## Overview

At 1-2, opponents rarely bluff for big money on the turn or river. Your first and most valuable post-flop skill is therefore a **folding skill**: disciplining yourself to release medium-strong hands whenever the action screams "I have a monster." Miller frames this as the single biggest leak-plugger in small-stakes live poker — you stop bleeding money not by winning more, but by losing less.

---

## The Core Rule

> [!quote] Miller's rule — short and long form
> **Short.** *"If someone makes a big bet or raise, fold."*
> **Long.** *"If your opponent has played in a way that suggests a strong hand range, fold all your hands that can't compete with that range."*

Apply it religiously on turn and river. If you cannot beat the range your opponent is representing, fold without a second thought.

---

## The 1/3 Bluff-Frequency Math

> [!info] Why "fold" is the default at 1-2
> Pot $100, opponent bets $100. You call $100 to win $200 — so you can afford to be wrong twice for every time you're right. **Break-even bluff frequency = 1/3 (≈33%).**
>
> | Opponent bluffs... | Correct play |
> |---|---|
> | More than 1/3 of the time | Call every time |
> | Less than 1/3 of the time | Fold every time |
>
> Typical 1-2 opponents bluff roughly **10–15%** in these spots. You'd be wrong 85–90% of the time. **Fold.**

---

## When Does This Rule Apply?

| # | Qualification | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Street** | Most applicable on **turn and river** — by then, players rarely cold-bluff a stack. |
| 2 | **"Large" bet** | A size you'd **see only once or twice an hour** at the game (relative, not a fixed $ amount). |
| 3 | **"Stack-committing"** | *"You cannot imagine your opponent folding after making the bet."* |
| 4 | **Flop exception** | Does **not** apply to small/medium flop bets — the flop is where players *posture* and "see where they're at." Flop bets are unreliable indicators of strength. |

---

## Turn vs. River: Outs and Revisiting Decisions

### Why calling the turn ≠ committed to calling the river

Miller calls the "if you called the turn you must call the river" mantra **hogwash**. The river is a **brand-new decision point**. On the turn you had ways to win — outs, the chance your opponent is overplaying two pair, the chance you were already good. On the river those ways evaporate: no more cards, and an opponent who now shoves a committed stack has shed the weaker value hands they'd have cheerfully bet one street earlier.

### Having outs IS a reason to call

When you can enumerate multiple ways to win — live outs to improve, redraws, plus a non-zero chance your hand is already best — calling a big turn bet can be correct *even when the represented hand beats you right now*. Combine draw equity with "maybe-ahead" equity and the math often works.

---

## Pre-Flop Application: 3-Bets and 4-Bets

At 1-2, opponents **rarely 3-bet and almost never 4-bet** — and when they do, the range is essentially **AA / KK / AKs**. They do not 3-bet "light" with A5s or T9s.

- Facing a 3-bet, hands like **66, QJs** and similar should **fold, not call**.
- Exception: **200bb+ deep** stacks can sometimes justify a call on implied odds, since set-mining payoff dwarfs the pre-flop price.

---

## Multi-Way Pots and Loose Games

When **3+ players** see a flop and someone fires **more than half pot**, fold top pair with a weak kicker. Big multi-way flop bets are weighted heavily toward **strong made hands and big draws** — the crowd forces "honest" betting.

Miller's narrative: a loose player opens for $10; with just one caller, a $20 c-bet into $31 can easily be a standard stab and you call your top-pair-weak-kicker. But with **four callers** and the raiser firing **$50 into $61** *plus* a middle player flat-calling behind? Fold. Calling requires a "low-probability parlay" — that the raiser is betting weak *and* that the middle caller has something worse than yours. Far more likely one of them has you crushed.

## Example Hands

> [!example] Example — A♦K♠ on a wet turn (fold the shove)
> **Scenario.** 1-2, $200 effective. Hero opens to $7 with **A♦K♠**, gets **four callers** ($35 pot). Flop **A♥7♥6♠**. Checks to Hero, c-bets $30, one behind calls, SB calls ($125 pot, $163 behind). Turn **8♥** — three hearts and a three-straight on board. SB shoves $163.
> **Hand.** A♦K♠ — top pair, top kicker.
> **Decision.** Call or fold the turn shove?
> **Recommendation.** **Fold in a millisecond.**
> **Reasoning.** A worse ace (AQ/AJ/AT) is just as scared of the 8♥ and would rarely shove $163 — $40 maybe, not a stack. SB's shove represents flush / straight / set / two pair — **every hand in that range beats A-K**. And typical 1-2 players aren't bluffing this spot ~90% of the time. You can only beat a bluff, and bluffs are too rare.
> **Takeaway.** Top pair top kicker is not a calling hand when the represented range beats you *and* the bet is stack-committing.

> [!example] Example — 4♦4♠ with outs (call the turn, fold the river)
> **Scenario.** 1-2 game. Opener $7, one call, Hero calls on the button with **4♦4♠**, BB calls ($29 pot). Flop **K♠Q♠4♥** — bottom set. Checks to Hero, bets $30; BB calls, opener folds, other caller calls ($119 pot). Turn **J♠** — three-straight *and* three-flush. BB leads **$70**. Call leaves $150 behind.
> **Hand.** 4♦4♠ — bottom set, with the 4♠.
> **Decision.** Call the $70 turn bet? Then what if the river bricks and BB shoves?
> **Recommendation.** **Call the turn. Fold the river shove.**
> **Reasoning.** On the turn, three ways to win: (1) **ten outs** to a full house or quads (any K/Q/J/4); (2) **4♠ redraw** — a fourth spade may give you a flush over their straight; (3) opponent may be overplaying two pair (KJ/QJ), meaning you're already best. Combined equity is too much to fold. On a bricked river, outs are gone, and a stack-committing shove on this board sheds the two-pair hands you beat — the range is now flushes and straights. Typical 1-2 players almost never bluff-shove this texture.
> **Takeaway.** The river is a new decision. Outs justify a turn call; a bricked river and a committed shove justify a fold.
