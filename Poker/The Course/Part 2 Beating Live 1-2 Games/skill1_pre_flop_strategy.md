# Skill #1 — Play a Simple and Effective Pre-Flop Strategy

*Companion document to [Part 2 — Beating Live 1-2 Games](<Part 2 Beating Live 1-2 Games (Main).md>)*

---

## Overview
Skill #1 is the foundation of beating 1-2: a tight, aggressive, position-aware opening strategy built around one idea — **equity-when-called**. Because 1-2 opponents play too many hands and telegraph strength, your edge comes from picking starting hands that flop something (a pair, a draw, a backdoor draw) often enough to keep betting profitably. Making big hands isn't the plan; having the right hands to bluff with is.

---

## Hand Selection: The Four Criteria
Miller organizes playable hands around four features. Suitedness is the gatekeeper — almost every non-pair hand he plays is suited. Big cards and connectedness are the two "qualifying" traits that, paired with suitedness, earn a hand into his range. Small pairs stand on their own as a separate category.

| Criterion         | Qualifying hands                                     | Why it matters at 1-2                                                                                   | Priority                                                             |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Suitedness**    | Any two suited cards                                 | Flush draws give fallback equity when your bluffs get called; flushes win the large multi-way pots      | Most important trait — majority of unsuited hands are "unplayable"   |
| **Big cards**     | A-K, A-Q (offsuit OK); K-Q, K-J etc. need suitedness | Top pair wins medium pots; overcards give equity vs. loose ranges                                       | A-K/A-Q are premium; below that, suitedness required                 |
| **Connectedness** | Suited connectors (9♥8♥, 6♠5♠)                       | Hit widest range of flops — pair, straight draw, flush draw, backdoors. Best bluffing hands in the deck | Substitute for big-card strength; offsuit connectors rarely playable |
| **Small pairs**   | 22–77 (roughly)                                      | Flop sets for huge pots; set-threat makes your bets credible on low boards                              | "Last-resort" post-flop bluffs — terrible equity when they miss      |

---

## A Quick Summary
Miller's rule of thumb: **aside from pocket pairs, a hand must be suited, and it must also have either big cards or connectedness.** A suited hand with one of those two features is often playable; a suited hand with neither is "rarely worth any money." Offsuit hands below A-Q are essentially junk.

---

## Pre-Flop Frequencies
Miller collapses all pre-button seats into a single "early position" bucket and gives a simple frequency ladder.

| Position                           | % of hands played (unopened)      |
| ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Early Position (UTG through 2-off) | ~14%                              |
| Cutoff                             | ~22%                              |
| Button                             | ~33%                              |
| Blinds vs. steal-raise             | Defend ~36% (with ~16% as 3-bets) |

**Fundamental rule, all positions:** if you play, you raise. No limping.

---

## Position-Based Opening Ranges

Think of the four positions as a **color-coded heat map** — as you move from early position to the button, the range heats up (tight → wide). The blinds are a separate blue zone because you're defending, not initiating.

> [!tip] Position cheat sheet — tight to wide
> | Position                   | Heat          | Frequency                      | Mindset                                          |
> | -------------------------- | ------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
> | 🔴 **Early (EP)**          | Cold / tight  | **~14%**                       | Many players left to act — only premium hands    |
> | 🟠 **Cutoff (CO)**         | Warming up    | **~22%**                       | One seat from the button — widen, but stay aware |
> | 🟢 **Button (BTN)**        | Hot / wide    | **~33%**                       | Best seat in poker — steal liberally             |
> | 🔵 **Blinds (SB/BB)**      | Reactive      | Defend ~36% vs steals          | React based on the raiser; do not initiate       |

> [!danger] 🔴 Early Position (UTG → 2-off button) — ~14%
> **Open-raise range:** *22+, A2s+, KTs+, QTs+, JTs–76s, AKo, AQo.*
>
> | Category              | Hands                       | Memory aid                                     |
> | --------------------- | --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
> | **Pocket pairs**      | 22+                         | **Any pair**                                   |
> | **Suited aces**       | A2s+                        | **Every** suited ace                           |
> | **Suited broadways**  | KTs+, QTs+, JTs             | K-T or better, suited only                     |
> | **Suited connectors** | JTs, T9s, 98s, 87s, 76s     | Run of 5, stopping at 76s                      |
> | **Offsuit**           | AKo, AQo                    | **Only AK and AQ** — *no other* offsuit hands  |

> **Facing a tight raiser:** 3-bet AA–KK (+ A5s as a bluff); flat QQ–22, ATs+, suited broadways, AKo.
> **Facing a loose raiser:** 3-bet AA–QQ, AKs, A5s–A2s, T9s, 87s, AKo.

> **Open-raise range:** *22+, A2s+, K7s+, Q9s+, JTs–43s, J9s–53s, ATo+, KJo+.*
>
> | Category              | Hands          | Memory aid                          |
> | --------------------- | -------------- | ----------------------------------- |

> **Vs. tight raise:** collapse back to EP range.
> **Vs. loose raise:** 3-bet JJ+, AKs, A5s–A2s + select suited connectors as bluffs.

>
> | Category              | Hands                     | Memory aid                                                 |
> | --------------------- | ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
> | **Pocket pairs**      | 22+                       | Any pair                                                   |
> | **Suited aces**       | A2s+                      | Any suited ace                                             |
> | **Suited kings**      | K2s+                      | **Any suited king**                                        |
> | **Suited queens**     | Q5s+                      | Q-5 or better                                              |
> | **Suited jacks**      | J7s+                      | J-7 or better                                              |

---

> [!info] 🔵 The Blinds (SB & BB) — defend ~36% vs steals
> Miller treats SB and BB similarly. You're not opening pots from the blinds — you're **reacting** to what's in front of you.
>
> | Situation                                | What to do                                                                                                                  |
> | ---------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
> | **Limped pot, SB**                       | Fold offsuit junk (J-4, 9-6). Complete or raise any pair / suited / connected / high offsuit. Raise-over-limpers: **99+, ATs+, KJs+, AQo+** |
> | **Vs. tight/strong raise**               | Don't feel obligated — use EP ranges                                                                                        |
> | **Vs. steal raise (loose CO/BTN raise)** | **Attack.** Defend ~36%, with a ~16% 3-bet range                                                                            |
>
> **Steal-defense 3-bet range:** 99+, 44–22, A2s+, KJs+, K7s–K5s, Q9s, 98s–54s, J9s–86s, AJo+, KQo.

## The High Points

1. **Play tight.** Don't stray far from the recommended frequencies.
2. **Avoid strength.** When a passive player suddenly raises, believe them.
3. **Attack weakness.** Your edge is that opponents enter too many pots with weak hands — raise them.
4. **Don't try to make hands.** "Making trips and straights and flushes is not how you win."
5. **Choose hands with equity-when-called.** 8-7 suited beats A-4 offsuit because it hits more flops — it's a better bluffing hand.
6. **Defend blinds against steals, not strong raises.** Fold to big bets; punish likely-weak raises with reraises and calls.

---

## Multi-Way Pots and Loose Games
Two flavors of loose game, two different adjustments.

**Limpers who fold to big raises.** Easy fix: just **raise bigger** ($15–$20 instead of $10). Treat the limpers as irrelevant for hand selection; size up to thin the field.

**Super-loose games where $20 still gets four callers.** Pots inflate fast, creating artificially shallow SPRs (often under 2 by the flop). You're committing a stack on the flop whether you want to or not, so:

- **Prioritize big cards and pocket pairs** — hands that hit a board *hard*. A-J, K-Q, K-T suited flopping top pair beats 8-7s flopping a draw when everyone's going to showdown.
- **Devalue small suited connectors** — their value is in folding equity, which vanishes in 5-way pots. Consider folding them to raises, or even making an exception and *limping* them.
- **Strip bluffs from your 3-bet range** — drop A5s and similar; 3-bet only for value, and expand value to include QQ, JJ, AK.

Warning: don't reach for these adjustments prematurely. Amateurs chronically assume "they never fold." Only abandon bluffs when you're genuinely certain you'll be called.

## Example Hands

> [!example] Example — Suited connectors over limpers
> **Scenario.** 1-2, $300 stacks. You're two off the button; two players limp to you.
> **Hand.** 9♣8♣
> **Decision.** Limp along and try to hit, or raise?
> **Recommendation.** **Raise to $10–$12.**
> **Reasoning.** The typical 1-2 mindset is "limp cheap, hit a flop, otherwise fold" — and that mindset has no edge. 9-8s is a premier bluffing hand precisely because it hits so many flops (pair, straight draw, flush draw, backdoors). Raising builds a pot you can steal later and disguises your range — when you do flop big, your aggression looks identical to how you'd play aces.
> **Takeaway.** Raise your suited connectors. Don't limp to "see a flop."

> [!example] Example — Big blind vs. a button steal
> **Scenario.** 1-2, $300 stacks. Two limpers, then an aggressive regular makes it $12 on the button. SB folds. You're in the BB.
> **Hand.** A♣4♣
> **Decision.** Fold, call, or 3-bet?
> **Recommendation.** **Reraise to $35.** (Fold and call are "okay," but the 3-bet is strongly preferred.)
> **Reasoning.** An aggressive regular raising over limpers from the button is stealing — his range is wide and weak (possibly down to 8-5s). The rule is attack weakness. A-4s has nut-flush potential, blocks his ace-high steals, and balances your range: if he sees you 3-bet A4s, he'll pay off your AA and KK later.
> **Takeaway.** Against likely steal-raises, 3-bet suited wheel-aces — they're the perfect blend of equity and balance.
