# Skill #3 — Assess Your Hand Value

*Companion document to [Part 2 — Beating Live 1-2 Games](<Part 2 Beating Live 1-2 Games (Main).md>)*

---

## Overview

Skill #3 is the mirror image of Skill #2: when your hand *is* good, your job is to systematically extract every possible dollar from weaker hands. Miller's central claim is that a good hand's only real worth is **showdown value** — you want to reach showdown *and* you want opponents paying on the way. The organizing question for every value bet is simple: **"what weaker hands will call me?"**

---

## What Weaker Hands Will Call?

Before betting a made hand, name the specific worse hands that would call. If you can list several (weaker aces, unimproved pairs, chasing flush draws), you have a value bet. If you can't, you're either bluffing or folding out everything you beat while only getting called by hands that beat you.

This heuristic does two jobs at once: it tells you **whether** to bet and **how big**. And because Skill #2 lets you fold easily when an opponent suddenly raises, you can bet aggressively for value without fearing the occasional scare card — you'll escape cleanly when you're beat.

---

## Streets of Value

A hand's worth isn't a single label ("top pair") but a count: **how many streets (flop, turn, river) will a worse hand pay you off on?**

| Streets | Typical hand | Plan |
|---|---|---|
| **3 streets** | Flopped set, strong two pair, overpair on dry board | Bet flop, turn, **and** river — plan sizing to get stacks in |
| **2 streets** | Strong top pair (e.g. A-K on A-9-4) | Bet two streets, check one — weaker aces fold by the river |
| **1 street** | Weak top pair (e.g. A-6 on A-9-4) | One bet max — beyond that only better hands continue |
| **0 streets** | Marginal pair on draw-heavy board | Check; hope for a brick, then reassess |

Estimating streets of value on the flop forces you to build a plan for the entire hand instead of betting blindly one street at a time.

---

## Which Streets?

Board texture dictates *which* streets to fire:

- **Draw-heavy (wet) boards** — extract early. Bet flop and turn; draws call while they have outs and fold on the river when they miss. Betting early also disguises whether you have a made hand or a draw.
- **Static (dry) boards** — no urgency. You can check any of the three streets to disguise strength, induce bluffs, or dodge a thin river call. Just don't always check the same street — observant opponents notice patterns.
- **Marginal hand on a wet board** — often check the flop entirely and hope the turn bricks. Once a safe card lands, you can value-bet confidently.

---

## Slowplaying

Miller's rule at 1-2: **don't.** Slowplaying specifically means checking back a three-street monster on the flop to "let them catch up." It costs you the river bet — the largest bet in the hand because the pot has compounded — and makes stacking opponents much harder.

When you flop a set, your goal isn't to milk one small bet out of air; it's to stack a guy who caught top pair or an overpair. That process **starts on the flop**. Checking one- or two-street hands is *not* slowplaying — it's pot control, and it's fine. And if an opponent bets into your monster, flat-calling is an acceptable concealment play because you still have all three streets available.

---

## Don't "Protect" Your Hand

The instinct to bet big with a vulnerable top pair to "make them fold their draws" is one of the biggest conceptual leaks at 1-2. The problem:

- **Folding out weaker hands** — the worse made hands you beat will fold to a big bet. You'd rather they called a smaller one.
- **Getting called by better** — the only hands that call a huge protection bet are the ones that already crush you (bigger pairs, two pair) or massive combo draws where you're actually behind.

A big bet designed to end the hand converts your made hand into a bluff — you're no longer reaching showdown. Bet *smaller* with vulnerable hands to keep weaker ones in, or check and wait for a safe card. Save the big bets for genuine three-street hands where you want to build the pot.

---

## Bet Sizing

Opponents have two hard thresholds: a strength above which they won't fold, and below which they won't call. Sizing exploits both.

| Hand type | Example line ($33 pot, $200 stacks) | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Three-street monster** | $40 flop → $60 turn → $90 river | Build pot early so river shove is *callable*, not an overbet |
| **Two-street value** | $20 → $30 → $40 (or check one street) | String weaker hands along without scaring them off |
| **Against tight regs** | Shade *smaller* | A tight player folds to $150 on the river but can't resist $50 |
| **Against loose callers** | Full-sized bets, sometimes river shove | They're already committed; charge them the maximum |

Common 1-2 mistake: tiny $10 flop bets with a set, leaving a $130 river overbet. Plan backwards from "all-in by the river" and size the flop to match. Don't worry about tipping your hand by sizing differently — at 1-2 the math gain dwarfs the information leak.

---

## Getting Value in Multi-Way Pots and Loose Games

In loose, multi-way games your strategy becomes deliberately **one-dimensional**: bet your good hands relentlessly. Because four or five players are chasing with middle pair and weak kickers, a standard top pair suddenly has three full streets of value.

The biggest multi-way mistake isn't betting too much — it's betting too little, then nervously checking down the turn and river because someone "might have" a flush. Miller's rule:

> When you flop top pair or better, you bet. You keep betting until someone raises you. When that happens, you fold.

Don't defend against callers; defend against raisers. Loose opponents will call a river shove with worse kings, stubborn jacks, and busted draws — so let them.

---

## Final Thoughts

Winning poker is more than "make a hand, bet it." It's planning the hand on the flop using streets-of-value logic, anticipating which turns and rivers help or hurt, and classifying the callers as weaker made hands vs. draws. The three skills lock together mathematically:

- **Skill #1** puts you in the pot with a stronger range than theirs.
- **Skill #3** systematically extracts value when that stronger range hits.
- **Skill #2** refuses to pay off when *they* finally hit.

Stronger hand more often + max value when ahead + no payoff when beat = a structural edge that's hard for a typical 1-2 field to overcome.

## Example Hands

> [!example] Example — Stacking a Set (9-9)
> **Scenario.** $200 effective. Two limpers, you raise 9♣9♦ to $10, both call. Pot $33, $190 behind.
> **Flop.** Q♠ 9♥ 2♦ — middle set. Checked to you.
> **Decision.** How do you size three streets to get stacks in?
> **Recommendation.** $40 flop → $60 turn → $90 river.
> **Reasoning.** Plan backwards from all-in. A tiny $10 flop bet forces a $130 river overbet that won't get called; $90 after a $60 turn bet *will*.
> **Takeaway.** With a monster, start building the pot on the flop — slowplay costs you the river.

> [!example] Example — When Top Pair Has Zero Streets (Q-T)
> **Scenario.** You hold Q♥T♦.
> **Flop.** Q♦ 9♣ 7♣ — top pair, dynamic wet board.
> **Decision.** Bet to "protect" against the draws, or check?
> **Recommendation.** Check.
> **Reasoning.** If called by a flush draw like A♣8♣, you're only 53% — small value. If called by K-Q or A-Q, you're crushed. Slightly ahead when winning, way behind when losing = no real streets of value. Wait for a turn brick before betting.
> **Takeaway.** Top pair on a wet board can be *worth nothing* — don't protect hands into folding out what you beat.

> [!example] Example — Shoving Through the Scare Card (K-T)
> **Scenario.** BB with K♥T♦, $200 stacks. Four limps + SB call; you check. Pot $12.
> **Flop.** K♣ J♣ 3♠ — you bet $12, three callers. **Turn.** 4♦ — you bet $50, two callers. **River.** Q♦ — pot $210, $136 behind. Straight possible, flush possible.
> **Decision.** Check and give up, or shove?
> **Recommendation.** Shove $136.
> **Reasoning.** A set or two pair would have raised the wet turn to protect. Flat calls point to weaker kings, stubborn jacks, busted draws — loose 1-2 players will call off with K-2, A-J, or even 5-5.
> **Takeaway.** In loose games, bet top pair until someone *raises*. Scare cards alone aren't a reason to check.
