# Core Strategy Philosophy

*Companion document to [Part 1 — The 30,000-Foot View](<Part 1 The 30000 Foot View (Main).md>)*

---

## Overview

Part 1's second foundational question: **where does the money actually come from in poker?** Miller's answer shapes everything else in the book. Money does not come from outthinking opponents or elaborate bluffs — it comes from systematically exploiting one specific flaw that most small-stakes players share.

---

## Where the Money Comes From

The central insight of the book:

> **Most small-stakes players play too many hands.** Wide ranges are weak ranges. The money comes from extracting value from those weak holdings — not from running complex plays.

- A player who enters too many pots will frequently hold **mediocre hands** on the flop, turn, and river.
- A disciplined player who enters with strong, position-appropriate hands will consistently have the **range advantage** — their holdings will beat the field on average.
- This edge compounds over time. You do not need to win every hand; you need to extract value consistently from opponents who are playing incorrectly.

---

## Play the Course, Not the Players

Miller calls the core strategy "**playing the course**" — executing a sound, systematic game plan rather than making adjustments based on individual table reads or opponent personalities.

- At $1-2 and $2-5, most opponents are not sophisticated enough to require complex counter-adjustments.
- A consistent, exploitative strategy beats the **player pool** as a whole without requiring you to profile individuals.

### The Two Rules

| Rule                | What It Means                                                                                                  |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Attack weakness** | Target pots where opponents likely hold mediocre hands (wide pre-flop ranges → weak post-flop holdings)        |
| **Avoid strength**  | When opponents show narrow, aggressive action (raise → 3-bet → large turn bet), they have a strong hand — fold |

---

## Focus on Intentional Actions

Every bet and call should be **deliberate**, not reflexive. Before acting, ask:

- What range am I targeting?
- What hands am I trying to get value from?
- Does my bet size make sense against the hands I want to call?

Reactive, emotional play — calling because you feel pot-committed, betting because you're frustrated — is exactly what funds the winning player's profit.

---

## The Three Pillars

The entire strategy reduces to three disciplines that run through the rest of the book:

1. **Enter pots in position with the right hands** — pre-flop selection and position determine the baseline.
2. **Do not pay people off when they represent strong hands** — avoiding big losses is as important as winning big pots.
3. **Know when your hand is good enough to want a showdown** — recognize when to extract value vs. when to fold.
