# The Poker Pyramid

*Companion document to [Part 1 — The 30,000-Foot View](<Part 1 The 30000 Foot View (Main).md>)*

---

## Overview

The **Poker Pyramid** is Miller's visual model for how hand ranges narrow as a hand progresses street by street. It is the structural foundation for understanding why certain bets should be respected — and why others can be attacked.

---

## The Model

```
         River  ◄ Narrowest: only strong hands remain
        Turn
       Flop
    Pre-Flop    ◄ Widest: all starting hands in play
```

- At **pre-flop**, any player can enter with any two cards — the range is at its widest.
- Each successive street (**flop → turn → river**) filters that range. A player who continues to bet or call is doing so with an increasingly **defined and stronger** set of hands.
- By the **river**, a player who bets large has essentially eliminated weak and medium holdings from their range. What remains is predominantly strong hands.

---

## Why This Matters

The pyramid has direct consequences for how you should respond to bets at each street:

| Street    | Range of Bettor                        | Your Response to a Large Bet              |
| --------- | -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Pre-Flop  | Very wide                              | Can re-raise or call with a broader range |
| Flop      | Still wide; many draws and weak pairs  | Can call with draws, pairs                |
| Turn      | Narrowing; draws or strong made hands  | Start giving more credit                  |
| **River** | **Narrow: strong made hands dominate** | **Fold if you only beat a bluff**         |

---

## The Core Implication

> **Most small-stakes players do not bluff the river.** When a recreational player makes a large bet on the river, they almost always have a strong hand. The pyramid explains why: to reach the river with a betting range, a player has called or bet on three previous streets — weak hands have been filtered out.

- If you call a large river bet and you can only beat a bluff, you are calling with negative expected value.
- The money you've already put in the pot is irrelevant — your decision must be based on **current equity** vs. their remaining range.

---

## Applying the Pyramid to Hand Reading

Use the pyramid to narrow opponent ranges in real time:

1. **Pre-flop**: What range of hands would they open/call from this position?
2. **Flop**: Which of those hands would continue given this board texture?
3. **Turn**: Which hands still make sense after the turn action?
4. **River**: What's left? Does their bet size match a strong range or a bluffing range?

The pyramid is not a formula — it is a **mental framework** for narrowing the possible hands an opponent holds at each decision point.
